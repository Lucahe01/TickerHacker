DoorDash: The Local Commerce Logistics Layer

DoorDash is the definitive leader in the U.S. local logistics and food delivery market. Its business is a complex, three-sided marketplace that connects millions of consumers with hundreds of thousands of merchants and a massive fleet of independent delivery drivers, known as "Dashers." While best known for restaurant food delivery, the company is aggressively expanding into new categories like grocery, convenience, and retail. DoorDash's strategy is to leverage its scale and sophisticated logistics technology to become the essential "last-mile" infrastructure for all forms of local commerce. This analysis explores DoorDash's powerful marketplace business model, its key performance indicators, and its strategic path toward long-term profitability.

Core Business Strategy

DoorDash's strategy is focused on growing its marketplace and expanding its reach:

  • Win in Every Category: Leverage its logistics network and brand to expand beyond restaurants into every local commerce category, including grocery, convenience, alcohol, and retail.
  • Grow the DashPass Ecosystem: Increase the adoption of its DashPass subscription service, which drives higher order frequency and builds strong customer loyalty.
  • Empower Local Merchants: Provide a suite of tools for merchants, including advertising, white-label delivery (Drive), and online storefronts, to help them grow their own businesses.
  • Drive Towards Profitability: Focus on improving logistics efficiency and operational discipline to grow Contribution Profit and achieve sustainable GAAP profitability.

How DoorDash Makes Money: A Multi-Sided Marketplace

DoorDash generates revenue from all three sides of its marketplace. **Consumers** pay delivery and service fees on their orders. **Merchants** pay a commission on the total value of orders facilitated by the platform. And **Dashers**, while not paying fees, are the essential third leg of the stool that makes the entire model work. The company's profitability is best measured by its **Contribution Profit**, which shows the profit per order after all variable costs are accounted for.

Consumer Revenue Streams

The primary source of consumer revenue is the fee paid on each order. This is supplemented by the high-margin DashPass subscription program, which offers members zero delivery fees and reduced service fees for a monthly or annual fee. DashPass is critical to the business model as its members are significantly more engaged and order more frequently than non-members.

Merchant Revenue Streams

DoorDash offers merchants a tiered commission structure. Restaurants can choose different plans (e.g., Basic, Plus, Premier) that charge a commission of 15% to 30% of the order value in exchange for varying levels of service and marketing support. Additionally, merchants can pay for advertising and sponsored listings to increase their visibility on the platform, creating another high-margin revenue stream.

Financial Deep Dive: The Path to Profitability

DoorDash's financial story is one of rapid top-line growth and a disciplined march towards profitability. The company has successfully scaled its Gross Order Volume (GOV) and revenue while steadily improving its unit economics, as measured by its Contribution Profit as a percentage of GOV. After years of GAAP losses driven by heavy investment in growth and stock-based compensation, the company has recently achieved GAAP profitability, a major milestone. The key metrics to watch are GOV growth and Adjusted EBITDA.

DoorDash has built a powerful competitive moat in the U.S. based on its superior scale and local network density. In a three-sided marketplace, the company with the most customers attracts the most merchants, which in turn attracts the most Dashers, creating a powerful, self-reinforcing flywheel.

Strategic Outlook: Risks & Rewards

The investment thesis for DoorDash is a bet on the long-term, structural shift of local commerce from offline to online. The rewards come from its market-leading position and its ability to expand into new categories, while the risks are centered on the intense competition and the uncertain regulatory landscape for the gig economy.
